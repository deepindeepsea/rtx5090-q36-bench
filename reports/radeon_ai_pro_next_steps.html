Porting q36's engine ideas to ROCm / RDNA4

Target: AMD Radeon AI PRO R9700 (gfx1201, 32 GB, ROCm 7.2.1) · Model: Qwen3.6-35B-A3B · Built on the R9700 + vLLM field series

The thesis in one paragraph. q36 wins on the RTX 5090 through five ideas. Two are hardware tricks unique to Blackwell (native MXFP4 tensor-core math, single-instruction fp4→fp16) and do not port to RDNA4. Three are architecture/systems ideas (whole-token graph capture with zero host sync, a hybrid attention-KV + SSM-checkpoint cache, and batch-bucketed slot scheduling) that port cleanly and — critically — are things no runtime on your R9700 does yet (llama.cpp, vLLM, SGLang all leave the decode dispatch overhead and the SSM caching gap on the table). So the R9700 play is not "beat the 5090" — the memory bus forbids that — it's "wrap the INT4/FP8 kernels you already built inside q36's zero-sync decode loop and hybrid cache," which is exactly the decode/agentic gap your Part 4 and Part 9 identified.

4 · The two hard parts, in detail

4.1 The expert GEMM — where the Blackwell magic doesn't transfer

q36's prefill lead (13.4k vs llama.cpp's 9.4k on the same card) comes almost entirely from feeding raw MXFP4 nibbles + their UE8M0 scales straight into the tensor core — no dequant, half the shared-memory traffic. RDNA4 has no block-scaled MMA, so you must pick your poison:

  • Path A — INT4 (recommended first). Requantize the MXFP4 experts to INT4 (AWQ) offline, and route the expert GEMM through the fused_moe_wvSplitK_int4_gemm you already built and validated on gfx1201. You lose the "zero dequant" property (there's an unpack), but you inherit a working, tuned kernel and native-AWQ loading path. This is the fastest route to a running engine.
  • Path B — FP8 W8A8 (higher ceiling). Upcast MXFP4→FP8 (E4M3) at load, pre-swizzle into RDNA4 WMMA fragments, run W8A8. Better numerics and uses the matrix engine fully, but experts grow 4→8 bit in VRAM and you add a load-time expansion. Worth prototyping once Path A runs.
Expectation: because neither path gets the zero-dequant win, R9700 prefill will trail the 5090 by more than the raw bandwidth ratio. Your llama.cpp HIP hit 3,491 t/s (pp512, 30B model) via rocBLAS; a pre-swizzled WMMA expert GEMM + mandatory flash-attention should beat that, but target the ~4–6k range, not 13k.

4.2 The gated-DeltaNet scan — the one genuinely new kernel

30 of 40 layers are recurrent (gated DeltaNet). On RDNA4 there is no llama.cpp/rocBLAS shortcut for this — it's the main new HIP kernel to write: a chunked scan where the intra-chunk part is a small matmul (WMMA-friendly) and the inter-chunk part is the sequential state carry. The good news: it's algorithm, not Blackwell hardware, so it ports as plain HIP. Check whether the vLLM/SGLang Triton "linear-attention / gated-deltanet" path (used for Qwen3-Next-style models) already compiles for gfx1201 in your SGLang tree — if so, it's a reference to validate against bit-for-bit.

8 · Where this actually beats your current R9700 stack

You already have vLLM, llama.cpp (HIP + Vulkan), and SGLang running on gfx1201. A q36-style engine isn't about replacing them — it's about the three things none of them do on this card:

  • Whole-token graph capture with zero host sync — directly attacks the "−15% HIP decode to dispatch/dequant" you measured in Part 4. This is how one HIP-based engine could match Vulkan's decode and keep HIP's prefill, collapsing your split-decision.
  • A cache that actually works for the recurrent layers — paged-attention runtimes silently can't reuse the 30 DeltaNet layers' state; q36's whole-state checkpoint does, which is the difference between a 13.6 s and a 0.4 s agent re-prefill on the exact multi-turn workload from Parts 9–10.
  • Batch-bucketed graphs + slot compaction — a cleaner path to your 450–480 tok/s aggregate than default MoE configs (recall Part 5's "No tuned config for E=128, N=384" warning).
